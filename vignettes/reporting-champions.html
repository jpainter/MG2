Champion Facilities and Reporting Bias

Overview

Aggregated DHIS2 totals are a joint function of two things: the number of cases that occurred, and the number of facilities that reported. When reporting participation changes over time — and it always does — the aggregate total changes even if the underlying incidence is stable. This is reporting bias.

This vignette:

  1. Demonstrates reporting bias in Lao PDR malaria surveillance data.
  2. Reproduces the two diagnostic charts at the top of MG2’s Reporting tab to show how champions are identified and why the date range of analysis matters.
  3. Compares the champion-facility trend against the raw trend to show how bias is removed.
  4. Shows how element selection and the strictness of the reporting criterion affect the champion set.

Prerequisite: The mechanics of COUNT vs. SUM in the MG2 API download, and why COUNT = 1 at the leaf-facility level, are covered in the companion vignette Facility Counts and Data Element Reporting in DHIS2. Read that first for the counting concepts that underpin champion identification.

1 Background: reporting bias in DHIS2

1.1 Why the facility count is not constant

In a national HMIS, the number of facilities reporting a given data element changes over time: new facilities open, others close, reporting guidelines change, and individual facilities may periodically lack staff or connectivity. The problem arises when analysts treat aggregate totals as if the denominator were constant.

A submitted form that is partially blank still counts as “complete” in DHIS2 — 100% dataset reporting coexists with 0% data element reporting. This distinction is explained in detail in the companion vignette.

1.2 The WHO guidance gap

Even the WHO Data Quality Tool, which internally uses field-level completeness, conflates the two levels in its documentation, stating that dataset reporting rates “will also tell you for which years there are data for the data element.” This is only true if submitted forms are never blank — an assumption that fails routinely in practice.

3.3 Indicator distortion from mismatched denominators

The problem is most acute when an indicator combines two elements — because the numerator and denominator may come from different sets of facilities. Test positivity (RDT positive ÷ suspects tested by RDT) is a standard malaria metric. If some facilities report tests but not positive results, or vice versa, the ratio is arithmetically meaningless.

4 Champion facilities: identification and diagnostics

4.1 Definition

A champion facility is one that reports a specified set of data elements in every month (or nearly every month) of a given calendar year. A time series built from the same set of consistently reporting facilities has no reporting bias — any change reflects a true change in caseload, not a change in who submitted data.

Champions are identified per year by mostFrequentReportingOUs(). For the main analyses below we use:

  • Element: MAL - RDT positive malaria cases (any age/sex category present, any_selected rule)
  • Date range: January 2024 – December 2025 (two complete years where reporting is stable; see Section 4.3 for why earlier years are excluded)
  • Threshold: 12/12 months required per year
# --- Identify champions: RDT positive, 2024–2025, 12/12, any_selected ---
champ_ids <- mostFrequentReportingOUs(
  d_c,
  data_categories = rdt_pos_cats,
  startingMonth   = yearmonth("2024 Jan"),
  endingMonth     = yearmonth("2025 Dec"),
  missing_reports = 0L,
  reporting_rule  = "any_selected"
)

cat(sprintf("Champions (RDT positive, 12/12, 2024–2025): %d of %d facilities (%.0f%%)\n",
            length(champ_ids), n_total,
            100 * length(champ_ids) / n_total))
#> Champions (RDT positive, 12/12, 2024–2025): 37 of 1534 facilities (2%)

4.3 Why the date range of analysis matters

The date range used to define champions is one of the most consequential analytic choices, yet it is easy to overlook.

mostFrequentReportingOUs() requires a facility to report consistently in every year of the analysis window. A facility that began reporting in 2024 will not qualify for a 2022–2025 window even if it reports perfectly in 2024 and 2025 — because it has no data in 2022 or 2023.

The Lao PDR data illustrates this dramatically. RDT positive reporting expanded about three-fold in 2024 (Figure 1). Many facilities in the 2024–2025 reporting system simply did not exist in the 2022–2023 system. Extending the window back to 2022 eliminates nearly all champions.

4.5 Effect of the reporting rule on champion count

The four reporting rules available in MG2’s Reporting tab change which facility- months count as “reporting.” Table 3 shows the impact on champion count for the same element set (RDT positive) and the same 2024–2025 window.

For RDT positive (which has 6 age × sex categories per facility), all_categories requires all six to be present every month — the strictest test. any_selected (the default) only requires that at least one category has a non-missing value. The any_data rule counts any non-missing data in the entire dataset regardless of element, making it the most inclusive.

Use all_categories when computing age- or sex-specific rates where an incomplete breakdown produces an undefined result. Use any_selected for burden estimation where the element total is what matters.

5 Element selection and the double-counting risk

5.1 A structural ambiguity in the Lao PDR data

The Lao PDR DHIS2 system records confirmed malaria cases in overlapping fields:

  • MAL - Confirmed malaria cases — an aggregate field (by age group), available from November 2024 only
  • MAL - RDT positive malaria cases — RDT-specific (by age × sex), available from January 2022
  • MAL - Microscopy positive malaria cases — microscopy-specific (by age × sex), available from January 2022

In this DHIS2 configuration the confirmed-cases field is meant to capture all confirmed cases (RDT positive + microscopy positive combined). If that is how facilities fill in the form, requiring all three as champion criteria asks for the same case to be recorded three times — and penalises facilities that correctly use only the aggregate field.

5.2 Empirical check: are the fields redundant?

5.3 Suspected ≠ Tested

  • MAL - Suspected malaria cases counts patients presenting with fever (clinical tally, before testing)
  • MAL - Malaria suspects tested (RDT) counts patients who actually received an RDT (laboratory tally)

These answer different questions. A champion criterion that requires both conflates the care-seeking and diagnostic pathways, and many high-performing facilities consistently report only one.

5.4 Recommended element set for Lao PDR

For estimating confirmed malaria burden:

Analysis period Recommended element(s) Rationale
Jan 2022 – present RDT positive malaria cases Longest consistently available series
Nov 2024 – present Confirmed malaria cases Aggregate field; check for redundancy with lab types first
For test positivity RDT positive + Suspects tested (RDT) Matched numerator / denominator

For all analyses, use any_selected unless every age/sex breakdown is required.

8 Summary and reporting standards

What to include in a methods section

  1. Which elements defined the champion criterion — e.g., “RDT positive malaria cases (any age/sex category present) or suspects tested by RDT.”
  2. The reporting rule — e.g., “any_selected: a facility was credited as reporting in a given month if at least one selected element had a non-missing value.”
  3. The analysis window and rationale — e.g., “January 2024 – December 2025, corresponding to the period of stable reporting system participation. An earlier start date (2022 or 2023) was explored but yielded zero champions due to the 2024 expansion of the reporting system.”
  4. The monthly threshold — e.g., “12 of 12 months per year required (zero missing months allowed).”
  5. Champion counts and share — e.g., “Of N facilities ever reporting, M were champions in 2024 and M’ in 2025 (X% and X’% of all facilities).”
  6. A representativeness statement — noting whether champion and non-champion caseloads are similar (Figure 8) and whether champions are geographically distributed across provinces (Figure 9).

Forward note: imputation

The champion-facility approach produces an unbiased total for the champion set. To estimate totals for all facilities, MG2 extrapolates by scaling champion- facility rates to the full expected-facility denominator within each geographic unit. A complementary approach — imputing missing months for non-champion facilities using time-series models — is covered in the evaluation vignette.
